(based on Mantle Hood, _The Ethnomusicologist_, 1982)

# Structural and Conceptual Foundation
## 1. Structural Architecture of the Organogram
An organogram is a composite symbolic structure, not a pictorial illustration.
It encodes multiple layers of information in a single graphical construct.
Each organogram consists of three principal zones:
### 1.1. Organological Core (Central Symbol)
The core contains:
- A family symbol (geometric base form).
- Internal segmentations or marks indicating:
    - subfamilies (genera/species),
    - morphological structure,
    - resonant body configuration,
    - number of functional components (e.g., strings, heads).
The base geometric identity must remain visually legible even after segmentation.
---
### 1.2. Inferior Attachment Zone (Technique and Support)
The lower region of the base symbol encodes:
- Mode of excitation (struck, plucked, bowed, etc.).
- Contact interface (stick, mallet, hand, heel, keyboard, etc.).
- Instrument support (ground, lap, strap, stand, etc.).
- Modifiers (idiophonic, membranic, electronic).
Reading logic:
What vibrates → How it is activated → With what → Supported how
---
### 1.3. Lateral Information Zones (Ethnographic Metadata)
When used, the left and right sides of the symbol encode:
- Hardness Scales (technical acoustic properties).
- Cultural valuation.
- Social function.
- Ritual significance.
- Status markers.
These are not acoustical descriptors but ethnographic metadata fields.
---
## 2. Family Symbols (Base Geometric Ontology)
Each instrument family is represented by a distinct geometric form:
|Family|Symbol Shape|
|---|---|
|Idiophones|Square|
|Membranophones|Horizontal rectangle|
|Chordophones|Vertical rectangle|
|Aerophones|Circle|
|Electronophones|Diamond (rhombus)|
These shapes function as ontological identifiers, not decorative icons.
Segmentation within these shapes produces subcategories.
---
## 3. Morphological Segmentation Rules
Subclassification is achieved through internal segmentation while preserving outer geometry.
Examples of morphological descriptors:
- Cylinder
- Barrel
- Cone
- Bowl
- Hourglass (as deformation of cylinder)
If both internal and external morphology are encoded, the markers are layered in order.
---
## 4. Instrument Position (Spatial Orientation During Performance)
Instrument orientation relative to gravity and performer:
- Vertical
- Horizontal
- Angular
- Variable
Each family has corresponding orientation glyph variants.
This is a structural descriptor, not symbolic decoration.
---
## 5. Performer Position
The performer is represented by a small circular mark (head symbol).
Height indicates posture:
- High → standing
- Middle → seated on chair
- Low → seated on ground
Additional marks encode:
- Multiple performers
- Variable number of performers
The performer mark is positioned relative to the organogram boundary.
---
## 6. Instrument Support
Support is encoded in the lower zone.
Primary supports:
- Ground contact
- Stand
- Lap
- Body strap
Extended supports include:
- Cushion
- Between legs
- Across legs
- Under arm (left/right)
- On shoulder (left/right)
- Chin
- Side support
- Hand-held
- Belly / chest
- Around performer
- Suspended (cord systems)
- Composite xylophone supports (rail, log, trough, etc.)
Composite supports may combine multiple symbols.
---
## 7. Modes of Excitation and Interface
Action-based symbols:
- Shaken
- Plucked
- Rubbed
- Scraped
- Bowed
- Keyboard-actuated
- Stick / padded stick / crooked stick
- Mechanically actuated
Body control markers:
- Left hand
- Right hand
- Left heel
- Right heel
These are attached to the excitation layer.
---
## 8. Resonator Encoding
Organograms may encode:
- Number of resonators (numeric marker)
- Common resonator platform (for sets)
- Individually suspended resonators
---
## 9. Modifiers and Hybridization
Modifiers allow cross-family hybridization.
- Idiophonic modifier → small square
- Membranic modifier → small rectangle
- Electronic modifier → small diamond
For electronophones:
- The diamond is the base symbol.
- Signal generation/processing elements attach to the left vertex.
- Control/output elements (including loudspeakers) attach to the right vertex.
This introduces directional logic into the otherwise static organogram.

# Technical and Ethnographic Encoding Layer
## 10. Hardness Scales (Technical Quantification Layer)
Hood introduces calibrated scalar fields to encode measurable acoustic or material properties.
These are not visual icons but numeric scales integrated into the symbol structure.
### 10.1. HSL — Loudness
- Unit reference: decibel (dB)
- Encoded on a 1–10 ordinal scale
- Represents relative acoustic intensity
### 10.2. HSP — Pitch
- Unit reference: cycles per second (Hz)
- Encoded on a 1–10 ordinal scale
- Represents pitch range or pitch center
### 10.3. HSQ — Quality
- Qualitative timbral continuum
- 1–10 scale
- Example conceptual range:
    - Globular flute → Cymbal (broad spectral brightness range)
This is an attempt to ordinally encode timbral density/brightness.
### 10.4. HSD — Density
- Unit reference: pulses per minute (ppm)
- 1–10 scale
- Represents event density or rhythmic compactness
### 10.5. HSM — Materials
- Material continuum scale (e.g., gourd → metal)
- Encoded 1–10
- Represents increasing material rigidity / hardness
---
## 11. Cultural and Social Metadata Fields
These fields appear laterally on the symbol and encode ethnographic context.
They are abbreviated and positioned along left or right vertical edges.
Categories include:
### 11.1. Social Status Indicators
- High-status / low-status group association
- Maker’s status
- Performer’s status
### 11.2. Ritual and Symbolic Function
- Ritual usage
- Libation / offering associations
- Magical power attribution
- Symbolic meaning
### 11.3. Value Encoding
- Social value
- Performer value
- Monetary value
- Lifecycle role
These are not acoustical but socio-symbolic descriptors.
---
## 12. Composite Symbol Logic
An organogram must preserve internal coherence:
1. Base family symbol defines ontological category.
2. Internal segmentation defines morphological subclass.
3. Inferior attachments define excitation and support.
4. Lateral fields define contextual metadata.
5. Modifiers define hybridization.
The reading direction is systematic:
```
Family → Morphology → Excitation → Control → Support → Metadata
```
No element should contradict family identity.
---
## 13. Electronophone Structural Logic
Electronophones introduce directional signal logic into the system.
### 13.1. Base Form
- Diamond (rhombus)
### 13.2. Left Vertex
Represents:
- Signal generation
- Oscillators
- Filters
- Processing modules
### 13.3. Right Vertex
Represents:
- Control
- Output
- Loudspeaker(s)
Thus electronophones encode internal signal architecture.
In hybrid instruments:
- A small diamond attached to a non-electronic family symbol indicates electronic modification.
---
## 14. Hybridization Rules
Hybrid instruments are expressed by adding modifiers rather than redefining the base family.
Example patterns:
- Membranophone + electronic modifier
- Idiophone + membranic modifier
- Chordophone + electronic pickup (small diamond)
Hybridization must not destroy base symbol legibility.
---
## 15. Directionality and Flow (Implicit Logic)
Traditional acoustic organograms are structurally static.
Electronophones introduce directional logic:
- Left-to-right processing
- Signal flow toward output
However, arrows are not originally central in Hood’s system; they emerge in extended interpretations.
---
## 16. System Properties
The organogram system is:
- Non-hierarchical (unlike Hornbostel–Sachs)
- Compositional rather than classificatory
- Graphical rather than purely textual
- Modular
- Extensible
It allows partial representation: only relevant layers may be encoded.
---
## 17. Structural Constraints
1. Family identity must remain visible.
2. Segmentation cannot obscure base geometry.
3. Attachments must be spatially consistent.
4. Metadata must not be confused with organological data.
5. Modifiers must remain subordinate to family symbol.
---
## 18. Interpretation Limits
Important limitations:
- Hardness scales are ordinal, not absolute measures.
- Timbral encoding is heuristic.
- Cultural fields risk oversimplification.
- Spatial attachment conventions require consistent training to read.
The system is descriptive, not ontologically absolute..
